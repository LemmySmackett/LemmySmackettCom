At the first light of a pale spring dawn, in a meadow where the dew beads shimmered on every blade of grass, two rabbits foraged. One of tawny fur, the other smoke gray, their names were Nettle and Sedge.

The older rabbit's weary whiskers twitched as she nosed through a tuft of long grass, sniffed a particularly promising sprig of newly sprung clover, and recoiled. "Oof."

Nettle glanced up, her furry cheeks bulging with the golden florets of a dandelion. "Hm?"

"Bit of wolf wee on this one," Sedge warned. "Best keep our ears up."

"Mmph!" Nettle swallowed. "Is it a clover?"

"Yes, and the scent is fresh. Too fresh. We should—"

"Are you gonna eat it?"

"What? No, I—"

Nettle hopped between Sedge and the clover.

"H-hey!"

"Well, if you won't eat it, I will." With one swift bite, the young upstart nipped the sprig, stem and all.

"Listen here, soft-ear—"

Nettle quickly chewed and swallowed. "Mother always says never let good clover go to waste."

"That right?" Sedge deadpanned. "And what does she say about fresh wee?"

"Mother says a little wee on the leaves never hurt anyone."

"Well..."

"Why, if we didn't eat our leaves because of a little wee, we'd never eat anything at all."

"This kind of wee can hurt plenty."

"It's a tad piquant but nothing special."

Sedge sighed.

"Now the mugwort over by the bear den, that's got a real kick."

"Listen!" Sedge hissed. "With a scent this fresh, the wolves can't be far. We best not tarry."

"Hm?"

"Let's head back to the warren."

"Why?" Nettle lazily turned and grazed on a tangle of chickweed.

"Why? I told you why. Wolves are near."

Nettle shrugged her little shoulders as she chewed. "I don't mind."

"You don't mind?" Sedge scoffed. "You'll mind plenty if they find you."

"I've got a right to be here as much as they do."

"Do you now?"

Nettle lifted her chin. "I do."

"Yeah? Well, their fangs say otherwise."

"Their fangs can say it to my face."

"That is the, uh, general idea."

"Truth told, I don't think anybody's ever tried sitting everyone down and just hashing this whole thing out."

"Whole thing?" Sedge blinked.

"Having a proper conversation."

"Conversation?" Sedge repeated in mounting bewilderment. "With a *wolf?*"

"They're perfectly capable of it," Nettle asserted offhandedly. "No different from you or me."

"Now hold on," Sedge protested. "Wolves and rabbits are plenty different."

"I don't think so. I've seen one from a distance, and I'd say we're plenty alike."

"You can't be—"

"Two ears, two eyes, a muzzle—"

"They call it a snout—"

"Full body fur and four legs—"

"Okay, but that's—"

"Why they even have a tail."

"A long one!" Sedge was quick to point out. "Nothing like ours at all!"

"And you hold that against them, do you?" Nettle asked in barely veiled reproach.

"I don't hold it against them," Sedge balked. "It's just biological difference. The natural order of things."

"The natural order," Nettle scoffed.

"And what you're observing is all superficial similarity!" Sedge went on, visibly flustered. "Why, the way you talk, wolves are practically rabbits."

"Well—"

"Temporarily embarrassed, at best!"

"Maybe they are."

Sedge blinked. "Come again?"

"Maybe wolves are rabbits," Nettle said. "And they just don't know it yet."

"Of all the—they're wolves!"

"So you say."

"Throat-biters and bone-breakers all," Sedge intoned with a grim familiarity. "Tireless, fleet-footed terrors, as cunning as they are ruthless."

"Burrow tales," Nettle retorted.

"Burrow tales!" Sedge cried.

"I say we're just the same. Furthermore, a wolf could be a rabbit if it really wanted to be—"

"Am I hearing this right?" Sedge scratched her floppy ear with her hind foot. "Are these tired, old ears finally failing me in my late age?"

"—if we simply gave them the chance," Nettle implored.

"Hold on, hold on," Sedge said. "You think the wolves have devoured our kind since time immemorial because we haven't given them a chance to embrace their inner rabbit?"

"It's possible."

"Is it?"

"How can we know until we try?"

"Well, you're gonna average one attempt per rabbit so—"

"You say that, but I heard from a rabbit across the babbling brook that they've had excellent success in opening channels of preliminary communication with the foxes."

"Preliminary communication?" Sedge wrinkled her nose. "What in the wide green world is that?"

"The simple fact is that if we don't try to make peace with the local predators, we're at risk of falling behind the times."

"Oh, please." Sedge rolled her eyes. "Those rabbits have wanted our warren for years—"

Nettle wearily sighed. "There you go again—"

"—they'd be more than happy if we delivered ourselves to a wolf den with bright red bows tied on the tops of our pretty little heads."

"—stoking fear and peddling conspiratorial nonsense. As usual."

"Tch, you're the one spewing nonsense. Wolves aren't rabbits!"

"You choose to see what you want to see—"

"Wolves are wolves!"

"—and I choose to see the rabbit in everyone."

"That so? Okay, let's see how far this goes. What about owls?"

Nettle tilted her head. "Owls?"

"They'll pick off a kit if they can. Are owls rabbits too?"

Nettle cocked her head, obviously thinking about it for the first time. "I suppose they are."

"Really."

"Yes," Nettle said, now confident. "Owls are rabbits."

"Even though they ain't got no fur or four legs. No snout or muzzle."

"Mhm. They're flying rabbits."

"Uh huh. And they just don't know it yet?"

"That's right."

"Okay, stoats. Weasels."

"Long rabbits."

"Of course. And eagles?"

"Flying rabbits."

"Just like the owls, huh?"

"Sure." Nettle shrugged. "One's a daytime flying rabbit and the other's a nighttime flying rabbit. Simple."

"So everything in the whole world is a rabbit?"

"That's right." Nettle nodded. "Everything is divinely blessed with a sacred, inner light—an inalienable rabbithood."

Sedge gave her young, tawny companion a long, hard stare. "You really believe that," she finally said.

"I do," Nettle replied with an air of absolute conviction.

"How?"

"I told you: I choose to see the good in everyone."

"No, you said you choose to see the rabbit in everyone."

"Same thing," Nettle said. "My mother says I have an empathetic soul."

"Your mother's taking the wee."

Nettle scrunched her nose. "Excuse me?"

"You ain't seein' them, soft-ear," Sedge told her. "You're only seein' yourself everywhere."

"Psh. If you want to call me a narcissist, you can just call me a narcissist."

"Okay. You're a narcissist."

"Tch! Oh yeah? Well, my mother always says you're a hoary old moss-back with crooked ears."

"That right? Well, that's the first thing I ever heard your mother say that wasn't pure crotch water, and I've known her for five years. Now let's head back before—" Sedge's ears snapped upright. "Wait."

The old rabbit froze as two lupine silhouettes emerged downwind from a bush in the meadow, not forty yards out.

"My ears are going bad," Sedge whispered. "I'll go left, you go right. If we head in two directions, maybe one of us—"

"I'm going to talk to them," Nettle declared as she turned toward the new arrivals.

"What?" Sedge whispered hoarsely.

"We shouldn't have to live like this."

"We won't be living at all if you don't Lower. Your. Voice."

"The youth cannot persist forever in the world the elderly have created."

"I was born in it, the same as you, ya daft nit! Now stop running your mouth and run your—"

"Hello!" Nettle shouted to the wolves. "My name is Nettle!"

The wolves perked their heads.

"Would you like to be my friend?!"

The wolves shared a glance before trotting toward the welcoming long-ear.

"Frith's Folly, you've done it now!" Sedge cried. "Run!" She took off.

"Oh, posh, look how eager they are to meet us!"

The wolves broke into a gallop, strings of saliva streaming from their foamy jowls. One peeled off to chase Sedge while the other barreled directly for Nettle.

"That is fast, though." Nettle's ears flattened. "Awfully fast. T-there's no rush really! I just wanted to say hello—"

Jaws wide, the wolf lunged.

---

A few minutes later, the two wolves trotted back across the meadow, toward the dark, dense forest where their den lay. Clenched tight in their jaws, by the nape of the neck, hung the bloodied, limp bodies of the two rabbits: Nettle and Sedge.

"Hey, Sedge." Nettle whispered. "Psst. Hey!"

"Please let me die in peace," Sedge mumbled with her eyes shut.

"They didn't kill us! That's good, right?"

"My hind leg is broken, and these murderous fiends are dragging us back to dismember us in their den at their leisure, so no—" Sedge opened her eyes. "Not good."

"Come on."

Sedge glared. "You wouldn't know good if it bit you in the face."

"I still think there's a chance."

Sedge groaned, as much from annoyance as the puncture wounds in her belly.

"Once we're out of these jaws, we can talk this whole thing out."

Sedge shut her eyes again. "Frith, take me."

"You know," Nettle said, "appeal to their better nature."
